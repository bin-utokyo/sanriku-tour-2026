## 2. Focus Site: Kamaishi Municipal Toni Elementary School and Toni Junior High School

Toni-cho, a coastal district of Kamaishi City, is located along the ria coastline of the Sanriku Coast and suffered severe tsunami damage following the Great East Japan Earthquake. Although the junior high school building on higher ground escaped the tsunami, it was still seriously damaged. The elementary school and children’s center near the coast were washed away. After the disaster, Kamaishi City held repeated discussions with local residents and decided to rebuild the elementary school, junior high school, and children’s center together on the former junior high school site. The project was developed through close coordination between civil engineering and architectural design, with the aim of turning the entire slope into a place of learning for children.

A distinctive feature of the newly reconstructed Toni Elementary and Junior High School is that it was planned not merely as a school building, but as a facility that integrates considerations of topography, settlement, evacuation, disaster prevention, and community revitalization. The buildings are arranged step by step along the slope and are designed so that people can move to safe areas, and then to even higher ground, through multiple routes. I was particularly impressed by the way emergency evacuation routes are naturally incorporated into everyday circulation. 

There are five buildings, including the children’s center, and they are arranged upward in stages, forming four layers as seen from the schoolyard. In addition, the second floor of each building is aligned with the first floor of the next layer, and the buildings are connected by corridors. As a result, the individual buildings do not stand independently, but together form one continuous space. 

This sense of continuity is not limited to the inside of the school. Toni Elementary and Junior High School is composed with an awareness of its connection to the outside, rather than as a closed school facility. Standing there in person, I felt that there were no conspicuous decorations or imposing structures, and that the school blended naturally and seamlessly into the surrounding community. A Professor explained that, the colors of the roofs and walls of the school buildings were based on the colors of the houses that once stood in the town. 

The details of the design were also extremely carefully considered. In an article by Jun Aoki [[1]](#references), it is pointed out that the school is not mechanically simple, but rather has a complex design in which dimensions and arrangements have been thoroughly adjusted. Toni Elementary and Junior High School received the 42nd Tohoku Architecture Award, Works Award in the General Buildings category. This can be understood as recognition of its high value as a place that connects an educational facility, a disaster-prevention base, and the local landscape in the reconstruction of a school after the earthquake disaster. 

The lesson from Toni Elementary and Junior High School is that disaster-prevention and reconstruction facilities should not be special places that function only during emergencies. Rather, they should be spaces that are used in daily life, familiar to the local community, and capable of guiding people naturally toward safer actions when necessary. The school demonstrates the importance of integrating architecture, topography, community life, and disaster prevention in reconstruction.

| Design aspect | Meaning observed on site |
|---|---|
| Terraced layout | The slope itself becomes part of the educational and evacuation environment. |
| Continuous corridors | Daily movement can naturally become evacuation movement. |
| Colors and materials | Roof and wall colors refer to the former village landscape. |
| Careful details | Mechanical equipment, handrails, terraces, and courtyards are treated without excessive visual emphasis. |

## 3. Lessons and Reflections

At Okawa Elementary School, the storyteller described the situation at the time in detail, and I felt my heart tighten as I imagined the confusion and fear at the scene. However, I realized that I should not simply regard it as a sad event, but think of it as something that could concern me personally. What matters is whether I can make decisions based on evidence and take action if my family or friends are placed in the same situation. As the words “The mountain will not save you” and “People cannot be saved unless they take action” suggest, knowledge and preparedness only have meaning when they ultimately lead to action.

At the Hamadori Regional Design Center Namie, I learned about smart mobility initiatives. I was impressed by the idea that what matters is not the technology itself, but how it is implemented in response to local issues. The same approach cannot necessarily be applied successfully to other regions.

Disasters can happen anytime and anywhere, and when they do occur, people may not necessarily be able to take the right actions because of group bias, confusion, or panic. For this reason, pre-disaster recovery planning supported by engineering is important. However, it must not remain only as theory. We need to understand the difficulty of changing human behavior, and the reasons behind that difficulty, and then translate this understanding into better systems. The same applies to accidents and injuries that occur around us. I continue to think deeply about issues that are directly connected to human life.

| Approach | Example | Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| Structural protection | Taro seawall | Large infrastructure can reduce risk, but may also create overconfidence or reduce visibility of the sea. |
| Living with the sea | Kerobe, Toni-cho | Some communities prioritize livelihood, visibility, and evacuation over a large seawall. |
| Memory and education | Okawa and Ukedo Elementary Schools | Disaster remains communicate lessons that cannot be understood through records alone. |
| Social implementation | Namie Smart Mobility | Technology must be deployed through dialogue with local residents and local problems. |
| Local energy resilience | Taro Solar Power Plant | Distributed energy systems can support both disaster resilience and local economic circulation. |
