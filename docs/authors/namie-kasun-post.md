## 1. Overview of Namie

### 1.1 Background

Namie Town, located in Fukushima Prefecture, was severely affected by the Great East Japan Earthquake in 2011 and the subsequent nuclear accident. Following the disaster, the entire population was evacuated, and the town remained largely uninhabitable for several years. Evacuation orders were gradually lifted beginning in 2017, marking the start of a phased reconstruction process. The town now consists of a mix of returnees, former residents who chose not to return, and newcomers, including workers and researchers attracted by new development initiatives.

Reconstruction in Namie has been characterized by a hub-based approach, with development concentrated in specific areas rather than evenly distributed across the town. In particular, the area surrounding Namie Station has been redeveloped as a central node for commercial, residential, and social activities. At the same time, the town is promoting innovation-led recovery, notably through the establishment of research institutions such as the Fukushima Institute for Research, Education, and Innovation. This approach reflects a broader strategy of “creative reconstruction,” which aims not only to restore the town but also to transform its economic and social structure.

## 2. Field Observations

### 2.1 Michino Eki and the surrounding area

The Michi-no-Eki functions as an important local activity and service hub within the town. As per the observations, it acts as a commercial and community hub, based on evidence such as the presence of retail spaces and local product shops, and Visitors stopping for rest and services. This suggests that Michi-no-Eki plays a dual role, supporting both local residents and visitors and contributing to economic activity and social interaction.

The Pokemon-themed park located near the Michi-no-Eki appears to serve as a symbolic and recreational space. It is likely intended to attract visitors and improve the town's image. This type of development reflects efforts to create a positive and welcoming environment, especially for younger generations.

### 2.2 Service Facilities and Ongoing Construction

Several facilities observed during the field visit indicate the gradual restoration and diversification of local infrastructure and service functions in Namie. It was observing the improvement of the service accessibility in the area based on the presence of postal services (Japan Post), supporting daily communication and logistics, Agricultural cooperative facilities such as JA Fukushima Sakura, contributing to local economic activities and agricultural support, Ongoing construction of new infrastructure, including EV-related development (EV garden), indicating investment in future-oriented systems.

These observations suggest that, alongside the development of central hubs, there is a parallel effort to rebuild essential services and economic support systems. The presence of traditional institutions, such as agricultural cooperatives, reflects continuity with pre-disaster economic structures, while new developments, such as EV-related infrastructure, indicate a transition toward a sustainable, innovation-driven recovery.

Furthermore, the coexistence of operational facilities and construction sites underscores that reconstruction in Namie remains an ongoing, evolving process, combining the restoration of basic services with the introduction of new technologies.

The Namie Design Center represents an important aspect of reconstruction in Namie, integrating creative engagement with emerging smart mobility solutions and collaborations with private-sector actors such as Nissan Motor Corporation.

Unlike conventional infrastructure, the Design Center functions as a platform for both social interaction and technological experimentation. The involvement of Nissan highlights how private-sector partnerships help introduce electric mobility and smart transport solutions, which are particularly well-suited to low-density environments.

### 2.3 Vacant Land and Low-Density Areas

Large portions of land across Namie remain vacant or underutilized. This is some evidence of extensive empty plots, Lack of active land use, and limited residential presence. This highlights the uneven nature of reconstruction and the emergence of a low-density spatial structure.

## 3. Impressions

**“Recovering cities…Preserving memories.”**  
*[都市を再生しつつ、記憶を継承する]*

This was my third visit to Namie, but it left a much deeper impression on me than my previous visits. While earlier visits allowed me to understand the spatial and physical aspects of reconstruction, this time I was more strongly aware of the human and emotional dimensions of the disaster and recovery process.

One of the impactful experiences during this Namie investigation was my visit to Ukedo Elementary School. Through the accounts shared there, I learned how teachers made rapid, critical decisions on the day of the disaster to evacuate all students to Mt. Ohira, approximately 1.5 km from the school. Standing near the memorial monument at Mt. Ohira, I found myself visualizing the situation, children and teachers running urgently to higher ground to escape the tsunami. This experience highlighted not only the severity of the disaster but also the strength, judgment, and responsibility demonstrated by those involved.

This visit made me realize the importance of preserving and transmitting these stories to future generations. Beyond physical reconstruction, it is essential to communicate the experiences, decisions, and resilience of local communities so that the lessons of the disaster are not forgotten.

At the same time, I observed that the people of Namie are making continuous efforts to rebuild their town and restore their livelihoods. Despite the challenges of population decline and spatial fragmentation, there is a clear determination to move forward. What stands out is not only the effort to recover but also the commitment to remember.

As I understand it, the phrase **“recovering cities…preserving memories”** captures the essence of Namie’s reconstruction. It reflects a dual process: rebuilding the physical environment and economic activities, while also maintaining a collective memory of the past. This suggests that recovery is not simply about returning to a previous state, but about creating a future that is informed by past experiences. Overall, this visit deepened my understanding that post-disaster reconstruction is not only a spatial or economic process, but also a social and emotional one. The case of Namie demonstrates that resilience lies not only in infrastructure and policy but also in communities' capacity to remember, adapt, and move forward.
