## 2. Focus Site: Namie Town

Namie Town, located in Fukushima Prefecture, was severely affected by the Great East Japan Earthquake in 2011 and the subsequent nuclear accident. After the disaster, the entire population was evacuated, and large parts of the town remained uninhabitable for several years. Evacuation orders were gradually lifted beginning in 2017, marking the start of a phased reconstruction process. Today, Namie includes returnees, former residents who have not returned, and newcomers such as workers and researchers connected to new development initiatives.

Reconstruction in Namie has followed a hub-based approach. Rather than restoring activity evenly across the whole town, development has been concentrated around specific nodes, especially the area around Namie Station and Michi-no-Eki Namie. This strategy creates places where commercial, residential, social, and visitor functions can overlap even while the wider municipal area remains low-density. At the same time, Namie is promoting innovation-led recovery through institutions and projects related to the Fukushima Institute for Research, Education and Innovation (F-REI), smart mobility, and energy transition. This reflects the idea of creative reconstruction: recovery not simply as restoration, but as an opportunity to reshape the town's economic and social structure.

### 2.1 Reconstruction Hub Around Michi-no-Eki Namie

Michi-no-Eki Namie functions as an important activity and service hub. The retail spaces, local product shops, and steady visitor movement observed on site suggest that it supports both residents and visitors. It is not only a rest stop, but also a visible marker of everyday recovery, where shopping, tourism, local products, and community interaction are brought together.

![道の駅なみえ]({{ '/assets/images/authors/namie-kasun-post/02.jpeg' | relative_url }})

![道の駅なみえ周辺のポケモン公園]({{ '/assets/images/authors/namie-kasun-post/03.jpeg' | relative_url }})

The Pokemon-themed park near Michi-no-Eki Namie also has symbolic value. It appears to help attract visitors and soften the image of a town often associated with disaster and evacuation. The park creates a more welcoming environment, particularly for families and younger generations, and shows how small recreational spaces can support the broader process of reintroducing ordinary life.

### 2.2 Services, Construction, and Smart Mobility

Several facilities observed during the field visit show the gradual restoration and diversification of local infrastructure. Japan Post Bank supports daily communication and financial services. JA Fukushima Sakura indicates the continued importance of agricultural and local economic activities. Ongoing construction, including EV-related development, shows investment in future-oriented systems alongside the recovery of basic services.

These observations suggest that Namie's reconstruction is not a single completed project, but an evolving process. Traditional institutions and new development projects coexist: agricultural cooperatives reflect continuity with the pre-disaster economy, while EV-related infrastructure points toward a more experimental and sustainable recovery model.

The Hamadori Regional Design Center Namie is another important part of this process. It functions as a platform for community engagement, design thinking, and experimentation with smart mobility. The involvement of private-sector actors such as Nissan Motor Corporation highlights how mobility solutions can be developed through collaboration rather than only through conventional public infrastructure.

![Hamadori Regional Design Center Namie]({{ '/assets/images/authors/namie-kasun-post/08.jpeg' | relative_url }})

In a town with low population density and dispersed destinations, mobility is not simply a matter of transport technology. It is closely tied to whether residents, returnees, visitors, and newcomers can use the town in daily life. Namie's smart mobility initiatives therefore raise a broader question: how can new technology be implemented in a way that responds to local social conditions?

### 2.3 Vacant Land and Low-Density Urban Structure

Large areas of vacant or underused land remain around the town center. The open plots and limited residential presence observed on site show that reconstruction has progressed unevenly. Even where evacuation orders have been lifted and key facilities have reopened, the town's spatial structure remains fragmented.

![Vacant land around the town center]({{ '/assets/images/authors/namie-kasun-post/09.jpeg' | relative_url }})

This low-density condition is one of Namie's most important planning challenges. Concentrating functions around hubs can make services more viable, but it also makes visible the distance between restored nodes and the wider areas that have not yet regained everyday activity. The result is a town where recovery is present, but not evenly distributed.

### 2.4 Ukedo Elementary School and Surrounding Area

The former Ukedo Elementary School serves as a memorial and educational site. The preserved school building, evacuation stair, surrounding open land, and memorial landscape communicate the physical scale of the disaster and the importance of evacuation decisions. The site also shows how memory preservation can be integrated with landscape planning.

## 3. Lessons and Reflections

**"Recovering cities while preserving memories."**

This was my third visit to Namie, but it left a much deeper impression on me than my previous visits. Earlier visits helped me understand the spatial and physical aspects of reconstruction. This time, I became more strongly aware of the human and emotional dimensions of the disaster and recovery process.

One of the most impactful experiences was visiting Ukedo Elementary School and the Mt. Ohira memorial area. Through the accounts shared there, I learned how teachers made rapid and critical decisions on March 11, 2011, evacuating all students to Mt. Ohira, approximately 1.5 km from the school. Standing near the memorial monument, I imagined children and teachers running urgently toward higher ground to escape the tsunami. The experience showed not only the severity of the disaster, but also the strength, judgment, and responsibility of the people involved.

![Mt. Ohira memorial monument]({{ '/assets/images/authors/namie-kasun-post/14.jpeg' | relative_url }})

![View from the Mt. Ohira area]({{ '/assets/images/authors/namie-kasun-post/15.jpeg' | relative_url }})

This visit made me realize the importance of preserving and transmitting these stories to future generations. Physical reconstruction is not enough. The experiences, decisions, and resilience of local communities also need to be communicated so that the lessons of the disaster are not forgotten.

At the same time, I observed that the people of Namie are continuing to rebuild their town and restore their livelihoods. Despite the challenges of population decline and spatial fragmentation, there is a clear determination to move forward. What stands out is not only the effort to recover, but also the commitment to remember.

| Theme | Observation | Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| Hub-based recovery | Activity is concentrated around Michi-no-Eki Namie and related facilities. | Reconstruction can become visible through focused nodes, but the wider low-density landscape must still be addressed. |
| Creative reconstruction | Smart mobility, EV infrastructure, and F-REI-related initiatives point toward new industries and systems. | Innovation should respond to local needs rather than being introduced as technology for its own sake. |
| Return and settlement | Returnees, non-returning former residents, newcomers, workers, and visitors coexist in different ways. | Recovery is a social process that cannot be measured only by rebuilt facilities. |
| Memory preservation | Ukedo Elementary School and Mt. Ohira preserve evacuation stories and disaster experience. | Remembering the past is part of building a future informed by disaster lessons. |

As I understand it, "recovering cities while preserving memories" captures the essence of Namie's reconstruction. It reflects a dual process: rebuilding physical environments and economic activities while maintaining a collective memory of the past. Namie demonstrates that resilience lies not only in infrastructure and policy, but also in a community's capacity to remember, adapt, and move forward.
