# Okawa Elementary School

## Overview

On March 11, 2011, Okawa Elementary School was struck by the tsunami generated by the Great East Japan Earthquake. The school was located about 3.7 kilometers from the coast and close to the Kitakami River. After the earthquake struck at 2:46 p.m., the students and teachers first gathered in the schoolyard. The tsunami reached the school at around 3:37 p.m., meaning that there was roughly 50 minutes between the earthquake and the arrival of the tsunami. During this time, they remained at the school while the teachers discussed where to evacuate. Shortly before the tsunami arrived, they began moving toward a nearby area known as the “Triangle Zone,” but the tsunami reached them while they were still evacuating

The tsunami reached the area not only from the river, but also from the landward side, engulfing the school and its surroundings. 74 students and 10 staff members lost their lives.

Some of the families affected by the loss at Okawa later argued that it should not be seen simply as a natural disaster, but as a tragedy that could have been prevented. In 2014, they filed a damages lawsuit against Ishinomaki City and the Miyagi Prefectural Government. In 2018, the Sendai High Court ruled in their favor, finding failures in both the school’s evacuation response after the earthquake and its preparedness before the disaster, including its emergency plan for a tsunami. The ruling became final in 2019, after the Supreme Court rejected the appeal.

In July 2021, Ishinomaki City opened the preserved remains of Okawa Elementary School to the public as a disaster memorial, preserving the memories and lessons of the tragedy and offering visitors an opportunity to reflect on life and on what it means to be alive.

## Field Observations

At Okawa Elementary School, Mr. Toshiro Sato guided us through the site and explained in detail what happened on the day of the disaster. Speaking as both a teacher and a family member of someone who died in the tsunami, his explanation was especially powerful. Together with photographs from before the disaster and the scenery before us, his words made us feel as if we were being taken back to that day.

![Fig.3]({{ '/assets/images/authors/okawa-huang-post/03.jpg' | relative_url }})

*Figure 3. Damaged Classroom Preserved at the Okawa Elementary School Ruins*

One important point was that the tsunami in this area was different from those in other places. Due to the influence of the bridge and the surrounding topography, the tsunami produced a backflow and whirlpool-like movements. Although there was a hill next to the school where people could have evacuated, and although evacuation drills had been conducted regularly, precious time was lost during the actual disaster because of misjudgments by a small number of people and the confusion caused by panic.

![Fig. 4]({{ '/assets/images/authors/okawa-huang-post/04.jpg' | relative_url }})

*Figure 4. The Kitakami River Bridge near Okawa Elementary School*

Another important lesson was that ordinary evacuation drills cannot simulate every possible situation. Unexpected conditions, such as blocked evacuation routes or power outages, are difficult to reproduce in advance. Therefore, even if evacuation drills are always successful, this does not guarantee that the same result can be achieved during a real disaster. Moreover, conditions differ from place to place, and actions taken in successful evacuation cases are not necessarily correct in every situation.

Finally, regarding how this place should be understood in the future, he stated that the lives that were saved are lives, and the lives that could not be saved are also lives. Therefore, this place should be regarded as a starting point for the future, just as expressed in the school song of Okawa Elementary School. It also asks us to consider what we can do for these young lives.

## Impressions and reflections

First, I realized that there may be a gap between how an event is presented through the media and how it is understood at the actual site. If we only rely on news reports, Okawa Elementary School may be seen simply as a disaster memorial that represents a failure of evacuation. However, the reality is much more complex. Reducing the event to such a simple explanation may itself cause further pain to the local community and to those affected by the disaster.

Second, this visit made me think about the importance of developing disaster prevention plans that reflect the specific characteristics of each place. Factors such as the possible direction of a tsunami, local geography, evacuation routes, and the disaster awareness of the people involved all need to be carefully considered.

Finally, I hope that more people will take the time to learn about this place, or visit the site in person if they have the opportunity. By standing there and trying to understand what happened, we can gain a deeper awareness of the importance of disaster preparedness and become more willing to take action before the next disaster occurs.
