## 1. About Kamaishi City

### 1.1 Overview

Kamaishi City is located in the central part of the Sanriku region in Iwate Prefecture, with a population of slightly under 30,000. Together with neighboring Tono City and Otsuchi Town, it is designated by Iwate Prefecture as part of the Kamaishi metropolitan area. The city is surrounded by a typical ria coastline, and nearly 90% of its area is covered by forests. The central urban district is located at the mouth of the Kasshi River, and residential areas extend along the river basin. On the northern coastal side lies the Unosumai district.

Traditionally, the city developed mainly through fishing. However, since the construction of a government-operated steelworks in the Meiji era, steel production has also become a major industry. At its peak in 1963, when the steel industry was thriving, the population exceeded 90,000. Since then, due to the decline of the steel industry and the impact of the Great East Japan Earthquake described later, the city has faced severe population decline.

In terms of transportation, Kamaishi is connected to Hanamaki City via the JR Kamaishi Line, while the Sanriku Railway Rias Line runs along the coast. Expressways have also been developed: the Kamaishi Expressway connecting inland areas and the Sanriku Coast Expressway along the coast. Notably, both roads include sections that were planned and constructed as post-disaster reconstruction and support roads after the earthquake.

### 1.2 Damage from the Great East Japan Earthquake

Like many other cities along the Pacific coast of the Tohoku region, Kamaishi City suffered devastating damage from the Great East Japan Earthquake on March 11, 2011. The city recorded a maximum seismic intensity of lower 6. Approximately 35 minutes after the earthquake, a massive tsunami—estimated at 9.3 meters at Kamaishi Port—struck the area.

The number of deaths and missing persons reached 1,064, corresponding to about 2.7% of the population at that time. A large number of houses were destroyed: nearly 30% of all housing units (4,704 units) were damaged, including 2,957 that were completely destroyed. One of the city’s major industries, fishing, also suffered catastrophic damage. Of the 1,732 fishing vessels, 1,692 (98%) were affected.

It should be noted that a massive breakwater had been constructed at the mouth of Kamaishi Bay before the disaster. This structure is considered to have delayed the tsunami inundation by approximately six minutes and contributed to reducing wave height and run-up.

### 1.3 Post-Disaster Reconstruction

After the disaster, Kamaishi City shifted away from relying solely on “hard” infrastructure such as seawalls. Instead, it pursued a fundamental transformation toward a broader concept of disaster resilience, emphasizing the ability of individuals, families, and communities to protect their lives through social connections.

## 2. Tenjin Public Reconstruction Housing

### 2.1 Overview of Reconstruction Housing

Reconstruction housing, also known as disaster public housing, refers to housing provided by local governments for disaster victims who lost their homes and are unable to rebuild them on their own. Unlike temporary housing, which is intended as a short-term solution, these units are designed for long-term residence.

In Kamaishi City, a total of 1,316 units were developed across 22 districts, and construction was completed in 2019. Various types of housing—from detached houses to steel-structure apartment buildings—were provided depending on the characteristics of each district and the required number of units.

Tenjin Reconstruction Housing is located on the northern side of the central area of Kamaishi City and consists of 53 units.

### 2.2 Design

This project was implemented through a “building-proposal-based public housing purchase” framework. In order to avoid delays caused by resource shortages and rising construction costs, a design-build procurement method was adopted. Private companies were responsible for both design and construction, and the completed buildings were then purchased by the government. This approach aimed to provide housing to residents as quickly as possible.

The housing was designed by architect Manabu Chiba and constructed by Daiwa House Industry. The design concept focused on creating a “distance” that allows residents both to be together and to be alone. While each building has a simple box-like structure, the arrangement of exterior corridors connecting the buildings was carefully designed to create opportunities for diverse relationships among residents.

Even corridors connecting the same buildings are shifted vertically between floors, allowing natural interplay of sights. At the same time, private spaces are secured in the deeper parts of each unit, maintaining a balance where residents can feel the presence of others without being forced into interaction. This project, along with Omachi Reconstruction Housing No.1 by the same team, received the Good Design Award in 2018.

### 2.3 Observations from the Site Visit

From the parking area on the north side of the building, the corridors of each block were clearly visible. As intended by the design, it seemed that communication among residents could be naturally encouraged.

This reminded me of my own childhood: after playing with friends after school and returning home, I would often make eye contact with my mother standing on a balcony or corridor, exchanging a few words from a distance. The openness of the corridors and the design of railings that allow visibility appear to contribute to such interactions.

On the other hand, since I visited on a Sunday, the area felt quiet and somewhat impersonal. Although my stay was brief, I could not observe the kind of organic relationships among residents that had been envisioned in the design.

For people who have experienced the hardship of losing their homes, fostering relationships among individuals who simply happen to live in the same building may be challenging. It may suggest that there are limits to what architectural design alone can achieve in creating social connections.

In the future, not only in disaster recovery but also in situations such as collective relocation from depopulated areas, there may be more cases where strangers move into public housing together. It is therefore necessary to continue considering what kinds of methods can promote community interaction in such contexts—and whether such interaction is even desirable for the residents themselves.

## 3. Reflections

Although I had previously visited the coastal areas of Fukushima and the Sanriku region, all of those visits were after the disaster. While I knew, as a matter of knowledge, that these places had been struck by an earthquake and tsunami years ago, this visit made me realize that I had not truly grasped that reality.

Traveling along the Sanriku ria coast by car or train, one repeatedly passes through tunnels and emerges into valleys where rivers flow and towns spread out. Looking toward the sea, however, one inevitably sees massive seawalls and towering floodgate control facilities. During my previous visits, I had perceived these landscapes as somewhat strange but had thought of them only as “the new normal” for Sanriku, from an outsider’s perspective.

This time, although my visit was brief, I carefully observed various disaster remains and disaster-prevention facilities. I strongly felt how closely intertwined human life, disasters, and disaster prevention are. Before the construction of seawalls, the sea was directly visible from these areas, and people’s lives were closely connected to it. Building a barrier between the sea and residential areas must have been a far more significant and difficult decision than those of us living in Tokyo can easily imagine. Reflecting on the process that led to such decisions was emotionally challenging.

At the same time, it is also true that memories fade over time. While the lessons of disaster prevention must not be forgotten, it is not necessarily a negative thing for landscapes with seawalls to become normalized.

Across from the Tenjin Reconstruction Housing, construction of the new Kamaishi City Hall was underway. Once the new building is completed and more residents begin to visit the area, the atmosphere around the housing complex may also change.

In order to realize the form of the city chosen by those who have decided to continue living here, I would like to think carefully about what I, as an engineer, can contribute.
