## 1. Overview of the Area

Based on the preliminary research conducted by 中澤さん.

![Map of Iwate Prefecture. Kerobe is the coastal city in pink.]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kerobe-laureen-post/01.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 1: Map of Iwate Prefecture. Kerobe is the coastal city in pink.*

Kerobe gets its name from the Ainu language, in which “Keroppe” means “gentle village”. It is indeed a peaceful village between the mountain and the sea, where the main activity is fishing. In the south part of Kamaishi city, facing Touni bay, about 220 people in 70 households live in the area, mostly engaging in the culture of sea products such as seaweed, scallops or sea urchins. There is a strong sense of community in the village, with an active neighborhood association.

The area was previously struck by the Meiji Sanriku Earthquake and Tsunami in 1896, with most of the residents part of the casualties. As a result, the memory of the tragedy was passed down through prevention measures such as relocation to higher ground and regular evacuation drills, which had a high participation rate (about 80 percent).

Therefore, when the Great East Japan Earthquake struck on March 11, 2011, all but one of the residents were able to evacuate to safer land. The 14.5-meter tsunami that hit the town was clearly visible before reaching the shore, giving the residents a sense of urgency that saved their lives. While human casualties were largely minimized, property damage was extensive: 25 out of about 70 of the households were washed away, and almost all the ships were lost.

The sense of readiness of the residents also shone through during the evacuation phase, when the abundant emergency food supplies allowed evacuees to eat their fill, and have a life as comfortable as possible with what they had despite the devastation.

During the recovery phase right after the disaster, the sense of community of the town and their dependence on the environment for subsistence motivated the residents to make decisions and start rebuilding sooner rather than later. The town chairman at the time, Mr. Shigetoshi Shimomura, led the reconstruction efforts of the town and gathered opinions about the best strategy for the reconstruction plan. Just one month after the earthquake, evacuation centers were closed, and the townspeople had reached an agreement about not building temporary housing or seawalls.

However, at the national level, in accordance with the Act on the Promotion of Tsunami Countermeasures (2011), the government promoted the construction of seawalls as part of an effort to reduce future damage caused by tsunamis. As of 2021, around 350 kilometers of seawalls have been built since 2011, with another 80 kilometers under construction, at a budget of ¥1.3 trillion (roughly $12 billion). The seawalls extend along the coast in a structure called “The Great Wall of Japan” by the media, with portions reaching a height of up to 15.5 meters.

In Kerobe, the construction of a 70 meters long, 14 meters high seawall would prevent fishing activities for up to five years, and drastically change the scenery of the town. The town chairman led the negotiations with the administration, conveyed the concerns of the fishermen, and explored alternative measures such as the construction of a road at the predicted inundation height and the designation of a no-resident zone below it. The local government agreed not to build the seawall in December 2011.

Once the plan was agreed upon, the construction itself was made difficult by the steep slopes and the bedrock soil, but the strong cooperation between the contractor, the town chairman and UR Urban Organization led it to completion. The disaster public housing was built with the lifestyle of the townspeople in mind, with for example water fountains for washing fishing gear built next to the entrance.

## 2. Tour Visit

During the tour, we went to Kerobe on the second day, between Toni and the Kamaishi Reconstruction Housing. We parked the car near the seaside and walked up the hill to the evacuation park. The road was very steep, and I could feel how it would be difficult to walk up in times of emergency. We tried to run, and ended up extremely out of breath. Older people or people with limited mobility might have some trouble. However, it did not take much time to get from the seaside to the evacuation park, so with appropriate training and regular evacuation drills, there should be enough time to get there from the evacuation announcement.

The town landscape is nested between the sea and the mountains. The road acts as a buffer between the residential area and the area where living is prohibited. In a sense, the hill and the road act as a visual and structural protection: with a clear delimitation and a steep hill, I got a sense of where the dangerous places were, and the path to safety is evident.

Being there also helped me understand the point that was made by the residents. In the other cities that we visited where a seawall was actually built, I often felt disoriented and unable to know in which direction the sea was. In the next decades, children who were born after the seawall was built might not get used to watching the sea, and they might have a false sense of security from being behind the wall. That’s why tours like this one and the work done by all the people we met during those two days is important. By making sure that the memories and the lessons learned from the past tragedies are passed down, resilience becomes more than just infrastructure, but also knowledge that builds a disaster preparation awareness as strong as walls.

## 3. Impressions and Reflection

The story of the recovery of Kerobe is interesting from several perspectives. It boils down to the characteristics of the town: the strong community rallied around a decisive leadership, and the townspeople's dependence on the environment for subsistence.

First, the residents of Kerobe were able to limit casualties and make the most out of their resources during the disaster, and then they managed to agree on a plan for recovery in a very short time. Because of this, when the plan from the government was revealed, they already knew what they wanted to do and took control of their fate. In doing so, they challenged the conventional top-down model of disaster recovery. After a disaster, to maximize the recovery speed and efficiency, decisions tend to be made at the central level. However, in Kerobe, the knowledge that the residents carried of their own land was leveraged effectively with cooperation from the institutional partner. Rather than a compromise, the final plan implemented was a superior solution that managed to achieve both the citizens and the government's objective.

Second, the town was very reliant on the sea, as most of the residents were fishermen. What could be seen as a vulnerability was also what gave them clarity and motivation to drive their own recovery. They had a strong environmental literacy that they wanted to preserve. By keeping their access to the sea, they also kept their ability to recognize the warning signs of a tsunami.

Kerobe's recovery was ultimately the result of a community that knew its people and its land.
