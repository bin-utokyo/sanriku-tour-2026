## 1. Overview: Rikuzentakata's Reconstruction and Kawaragawa Park

Rikuzentakata City was one of the areas severely damaged by the tsunami during the Great East Japan Earthquake, with approximately 1,761 victims, the largest number in Iwate Prefecture. Factors behind the scale of the damage include the fact that the urban area extended close to the coast, awareness of risk was relatively low because past tsunami damage had been comparatively limited, and designated evacuation shelters themselves were also damaged.

After the disaster, Rikuzentakata advanced its reconstruction under the concept of “creating a new coastal city in harmony with the sea, greenery, and the sun.” A defining feature of this reconstruction is the idea of multi-layered defense, combining seawalls, raised ground, and evacuation. The disaster prevention design combines hard and soft measures: seawalls respond to more frequent tsunamis (L1), raised ground responds to larger tsunamis (L2), and evacuation to higher ground is used for tsunamis exceeding those levels.

In the central urban area in particular, a large-scale land-raising project created ground approximately 10 meters higher than before. Commercial facilities, housing, and public facilities were concentrated on this raised land, rebuilding a “compact and convenient town.” Shopping facilities and a library were also placed as commercial anchors, and the urban design was planned with pedestrian circulation in mind. At the same time, challenges have also been pointed out, including the expansion of the project scale, population decline, and an increase in vacant lots.

Within this reconstruction plan, Kawaragawa Park was developed as an important space connecting the raised city center with the river. To resolve the large difference in elevation, the park adopts gentle slopes and a terraced spatial composition, allowing users to choose different heights freely as places to stay. In the river improvement project, the focus was not only on flood control functions but also on preserving the “river-like” character of the space. Consideration was given to water accessibility and ecosystems through measures such as gently sloped revetments, stone masonry revetments, and the introduction of a low-flow channel.

In addition, the design incorporates efforts to recreate, as much as possible, the pre-disaster topography and memories of the town. Rather than simply restoring what existed before, the spatial design is characterized by an emphasis on “passing down memory.” In this way, Rikuzentakata's reconstruction is a comprehensive initiative integrating disaster prevention, urban planning, landscape design, and the inheritance of memory, offering important implications as a model for future reconstruction.

## 2. Main Places Visited During the Tour

During this tour, we visited the central urban area of Rikuzentakata City and the surrounding reconstruction areas. The raised city center, the commercial facilities around Abasse Takata, and Kawaragawa Park were especially impressive.

In the central urban area, commercial and public facilities were concentrated on the flat urban space created through large-scale land raising, and it was possible to observe how the area had been reconstructed as a “compact city.” Efforts had also been made to create a sense of unity as a shopping district, including the development of public parking lots and the unified design of streets.

## 3. Kawaragawa Park

Kawaragawa Park was developed as a space connecting the raised city center with the river, and it plays an important role as a “place that connects the city and nature.”

What was particularly distinctive was the design for resolving the elevation difference created by the raised ground. The park is composed in terraces, with multiple levels such as a paved plaza on the upper level, a lawn plaza on the middle level, and a waterside space on the lower level. This allows users to choose where to stay according to their purposes, making the park function not merely as a passageway but as a “place to spend time.”

In the river improvement as well, consideration was given to the natural environment and landscape through stone masonry, gently sloped revetments, and the use of vegetation, rather than relying on simple concrete revetments.

## 4. Impressions and Reflections

### 4.1 A Design That Resolves the “Separation” Between City and Nature

What impressed me most was that the river and the city center were not separated from each other. Usually, when disaster prevention is prioritized, cities and waterfronts tend to be divided by high revetments or embankments. At Kawaragawa Park, however, the two are designed as an integrated space through gentle connections. I felt that this kind of design offers one answer to how disaster prevention and comfort can be compatible.

### 4.2 The Perspective of “Passing Down Memory”

Within the park, the design incorporates the orientation and structure of roads before the disaster, preserving memories of the former town. This conveyed an intention not simply to create a new town, but to pass the experience of the disaster on to the future.

In reconstruction, there is often debate over whether to “restore things as they were” or “rebuild them anew.” Kawaragawa Park can be understood as occupying a position between these two approaches, playing an important role in “reconstructing while preserving memory.”

### 4.3 Integration of Disaster Prevention and Spatial Design

In the improvement of the Kawaragawa River, not only flood control functions but also ecosystem conservation and improved access to the water were considered. For example, the installation of a low-flow channel adjusts water depth and flow velocity, creating conditions in which the natural environment can be more easily maintained.

In this way, the project is not simply infrastructure development; it is very distinctive in that it is designed as a “space used by people.” I felt that this will also be an important perspective in future urban planning.
