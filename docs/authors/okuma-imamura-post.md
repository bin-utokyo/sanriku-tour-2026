## Okuma Reconstruction Report

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Before the Disaster

Okuma Town, located in the Hamadori region of Fukushima Prefecture, was once known for its rich natural environment and close community ties. Since the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant began operation in the 1970s, the plant had played a central role in local employment and industry, and the town developed alongside it.


### 1.2 The Earthquake, Tsunami, and Nuclear Accident 

On March 11, 2011, Okuma Town was struck by the Great East Japan Earthquake, recording a seismic intensity of upper 6. The coastal area was then hit by tsunami waves over 10 meters high, causing severe 
damage. The following morning, all residents were ordered to evacuate to places such as Tamura City and nearby areas. While evacuating, residents watched the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant suffer a series of hydrogen explosions between March 12 and 15. These explosions released radioactive materials over a wide area and led to serious contamination. In April 2011, about three weeks after the disaster, the town relocated its municipal functions to Aizuwakamatsu City in anticipation of a long evacuation period. Residents were scattered not only across Fukushima but across 41 prefectures in Japan. In December 2012, the government reorganized the town into different zones based on radiation levels (Fig.1). Most residential areas were classified as places where return would be difficult. With the town left deserted and access restricted, homes and land gradually deteriorated, causing deep emotional pain for residents.

### 1.3 Reconstruction and Recovery 

Okuma Town decided to accept the Interim Storage Facility for contaminated soil and waste from decontamination work in Fukushima Prefecture. This meant accepting that much of the 
hometown landscape would be lost and that return to some areas would remain impossible for at least 30 years. Despite this hardship, the town made the decision in order to support the broader recovery of Fukushima. Since 2019, Okuma Town has moved steadily toward reconstruction through the phased lifting of evacuation orders. In 2019, restrictions were lifted in the Okawara and Nakayashiki districts, and in 2022, areas around Ono Station were also reopened. As a result, the lifted area expanded to about half of the town.
The new town hall, opened in Okawara Reconstruction District in 2019, became a symbol of recovery and hope. At the same time, decontamination, demolition, and infrastructure development have continued in areas where residents hope to return, and the number of people living in the town has gradually increased.

## 2. Places We Visited During the Tour 

### 2.1 Okumart: A Symbol of Everyday Recovery

We also visited Okumart in Okuma Town (Fig.3).
It is not just a shopping facility, but a place that supports daily life and shows the progress of reconstruction. With shops and services such as food, daily goods, and other essentials, it helps residents rebuild ordinary life in the town. Okumart also has an important role as a place of exchange. It welcomes not only residents but also visitors, creating opportunities for people to gather and interact. In this sense, the facility represents a visible form of recovery: it shows that Okuma is becoming a town where people can once again live, visit, and spend time together.
From the viewpoint of design, Okumart is open and accessible, making it easy for people to stop by casually. This kind of space is important in reconstruction because recovery is not only about rebuilding infrastructure, but also about restoring a sense of daily life and community.

![Fig.3]({{ '/assets/images/authors/okuma-imamura-post/03.png' | relative_url }})

### 2.2 Public House: The Foundation for Return

We also visited the disaster public housing in Okuma Town. This housing was developed for people who wish to return to the town after the disaster, so it plays a very important role in reconstruction. 
The housing is designed to provide not only safety and 
comfort but also a sense of community. Its low-rise style and calm appearance create an environment that feels more like a neighborhood than a large housing complex. This is meaningful because reconstruction in Okuma is closely connected to restoring familiar ways of living.
In addition, the housing is located near other important facilities such as the town hall and exchange spaces. 
This shows that reconstruction in Okuma is being carried out in an integrated way, linking housing, daily services, and community spaces. Infrastructure development in the area also reflects careful consideration for both safety and landscape. For example, utility poles have been removed to ensure they will not obstruct evacuation in the event of a future disaster (Fig.4), and agricultural waterways have been designed in a way that creates a pleasant stream-like atmosphere (Fig.5). Therefore, disaster public housing serves not only as a residential facility, but also as a foundation for rebuilding local life, human connections, and a safe as well as attractive living environment.

### 2.3 Manabiya Yume no Mori

Manabiya Yume no Mori plays a central role in Okuma’s recovery as a place where children can once again learn and grow inside the town (Fig.6). After the nuclear accident, education for Okuma’s children continued outside the town for many years, but educational activities restarted in Okuma in 2023. This means that an essential part of daily life has returned, showing that recovery has entered a new stage. 
It is also more than just a school. The facility helps create a safe environment for raising children, which supports residents’ return and long-term settlement. Okuma officially describes it as a community core that provides a shared learning environment for children aged 0 to 15 and a place where families can raise children with安心. 
In addition, Manabiya Yume no Mori has strong symbolic value. At the opening ceremony of the new school building, the mayor expressed the hope that it would be loved as a symbol of recovery and become a place where people in the community can continue learning together.

## 3. Discussion

Okuma Town’s reconstruction is significant because it is not simply a process of restoring what existed before the disaster. It is a process of rebuilding the conditions for people to live, gather, and create a future after losing their homes, community, and familiar landscape due to the earthquake, tsunami, and nuclear accident. In this sense, reconstruction in Okuma should be understood not only as physical recovery, but also as social and emotional recovery.
The history of Okuma shows that reconstruction has involved painful choices. The acceptance of the Interim Storage Facility is one example. This decision meant that some areas could not immediately return to their former condition, and that part of the hometown landscape would be changed for a long time. However, this also shows that Okuma’s reconstruction has been closely linked to the wider recovery of Fukushima as a whole. The town has had to move forward while balancing local loss and broader public responsibility.
At the same time, the reconstruction of Okuma is visible in the creation of everyday spaces such as Okuma Mart and disaster public housing. Okuma Mart is important because it restores ordinary life, offering a place for shopping, eating, and interaction. Disaster public housing is equally important because it provides the foundation for return and settlement. These facilities show that recovery is not achieved only by lifting evacuation orders or rebuilding infrastructure. It also requires places where people can live, feel comfortable, and reconnect with others.
Another important point is that Okuma is not aiming only to reproduce the town exactly as it was before 2011. Instead, it is trying to build a more sustainable and future-oriented community, as seen in its zero-carbon vision and integrated urban development. This suggests that reconstruction in Okuma has two meanings: recovering from the disaster and using that recovery as an opportunity to create a new model of town development.
Therefore, Okuma Town’s reconstruction can be understood as a long-term effort to transform disaster, loss, and displacement into a new form of community. Its experience reminds us that true reconstruction is not completed when buildings are rebuilt, but when people are once again able to live with dignity, connection, and hope.
