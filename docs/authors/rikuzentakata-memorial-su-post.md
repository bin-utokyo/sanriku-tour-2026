Theme: Understanding the Fukushima area after the Great East Japan Earthquake and
tsunami of March 11, 2011, and learning what reconstruction means for people, history, and
civil engineering.

1. General Feeling and Experience of the Whole Tour
This Fukushima tour was a meaningful chance to understand the affected area after the Great
East Japan Earthquake and tsunami of March 11, 2011. Before joining the tour, I mostly knew
the disaster through news reports, classroom explanations, and historical materials. However,
visiting the area made it much more real. I felt that a disaster is not only a past event, because
its influence remains in the landscape, in local memories, and in the long process of
reconstruction.
My overall feeling during the tour was a mixture of sadness, respect, and responsibility. I felt
sadness because so many lives were changed in a very short time, and respect because local
people and communities have continued moving forward even after such severe loss. At the
same time, I felt responsibility, because this tour was not only about remembering the past. It
was also about learning how we should protect people better in the future.
The museum was one of the strongest parts of the tour. We watched real videos showing the
short time before the tsunami and the destruction after it arrived. The footage showed that the
tsunami was extremely high and that it destroyed houses, buildings, and open plains in only a
few seconds. Seeing this process on screen made the disaster feel immediate and human
rather than distant and abstract.
The museum experience also explained why people had to move to higher ground
immediately. When the tsunami arrived, the available time was very short, and delay could
become fatal. Many people lost their homes, and many familiar places disappeared. Because
of that history, the lower area today is mainly used as memorial space, while residential areas
have been placed on higher land in order to reduce future tsunami damage. This idea of
separating remembrance space from safer living space is one of the clearest lessons I learned
from the tour.

2. What We Should Learn from Reconstruction and the Tsunami
One of the most important questions from this tour is why some areas and some people
survived while others did not. The answer is not only one factor. Survival depends on
topography, elevation, distance from the sea, evacuation timing, warning systems,
transportation access, and whether people had practical knowledge from past tsunamis. This
means disaster risk is shaped by both physical and social conditions.
The tsunami is especially important when learning from the past because it shows that nature
can go beyond human expectations. Even when protective infrastructure exists, people still
need clear evacuation routes, accurate information, and repeated disaster education.
Historical memory also matters. If communities forget past disasters, the same mistakes may
be repeated. In that sense, history is not separate from planning. Remembering earlier damage
is part of future preparedness.
Reconstruction must also be understood from different viewpoints. Technically, it includes
rebuilding roads, housing, lifelines, and coastal protection. Socially, it includes supporting
residents, preserving communities, and making sure recovery plans fit people’s needs. What
impressed me most is that recovery is not simply restoring the old town. It also means
rethinking how towns should be planned so that communities can be safer in the future.
This is why the memorial park, the coastline, and the preserved remains are all important
together. They show not only what was damaged, but also how planning decisions changed
after the disaster. Safer land use, evacuation awareness, and respect for historical memory all
became part of reconstruction. For me, this section of the tour made it clear that rebuilding
after a disaster should always combine engineering, history, and human experience.

3. Responsible Area: Rikuzentakata Memorial Park and Lessons for Civil Engineers
My responsible area is Rikuzentakata Memorial Park. This place left a strong impression on
me because it is not only a physical space, but also a place where memory, education, and
reconstruction are connected. A memorial park preserves the reality of what happened and
reminds visitors that the disaster should never be reduced to numbers alone. Behind every
record are people, families, neighborhoods, and ordinary daily lives that were deeply affected.
One especially important symbol in this area is the Miracle Pine. Even after enormous
destruction, this tree became a powerful reminder of survival, memory, and the emotional
weight of the site. Seeing the Miracle Pine made the disaster feel more concrete to me,
because it connected a very large historical event to one unforgettable image. In the same way,
preserved damaged buildings also communicate the force of the tsunami very directly. They
show that memory is not only emotional, but also physical and spatial.
From an engineering perspective, the memorial park is also a place of public learning. It
encourages visitors to think about hazard awareness, evacuation, land use, and the need to
preserve disaster history. For me, this area shows that reconstruction must include memory as
well as physical rebuilding.
As civil engineers, we should help create safer systems before disasters through hazard
assessment, better land-use planning, clear evacuation routes, and realistic preparation based
on history. After disasters, we should help restore lifelines quickly, support fair recovery, and
listen to the needs of affected communities. This tour made me feel that engineering should
combine safety, speed, memory, and empathy. If we learn carefully from places like
Rikuzentakata Memorial Park, we can contribute to protecting more people in the future.
