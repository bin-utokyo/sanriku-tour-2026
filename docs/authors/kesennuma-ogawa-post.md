## 1. Areas Visited

During the tour, we visited Asahi-cho, Shiomi-cho, Benten-cho, Uoichiba-mae, Minato-cho, Minamicho Kaigan, Uo-machi, and Uohama-cho, proceeding southward along Kesennuma Bay. Kesennuma is a city located on the southern side of the Sanriku Rias Coast that has developed with the fishing industry as its core. However, the coastal lowlands suffered devastating damage in the Great East Japan Earthquake. Under the post-disaster recovery land use plan, these areas are organized into the following functions within Kesennuma City: 1. Fishery processing industry hub, 2. Tourism and exchange hub, and 3. Central commercial hub. Specifically, Asahi-cho, Shiomi-cho, Benten-cho, and Uoichiba-mae fall under category 1; Minato-cho, Minamicho Kaigan, and Uohama-cho under category 2; and Uo-machi under category 3.

| Function | Main Districts | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Fishery Processing Industry Hub | Asahi-cho, Shiomi-cho, Benten-cho, Uoichiba-mae | Center of production and distribution: fishing port, fish market, fishery processing, and port logistics |
| Tourism and Exchange Hub | Minato-cho, Minamicho Kaigan, Uohama-cho | Place for exchange and staying, utilizing the inner bay landscape |
| Central Commercial Hub | Uo-machi | Near the central urban area; overlap of commerce and landscape formation |

## 2. Coexistence with the Sea

### 2.1 Measures for Continuing the Fishing Industry

![Kesennuma Fishing Port]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kesennuma-ogawa-post/01.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 1: Kesennuma Fishing Port*

![The current fish market. A seawall has been built on the landward side, but a floodgate is provided to ensure workability.]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kesennuma-ogawa-post/02.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 2: The current fish market. A seawall has been built on the landward side, but a floodgate is provided to ensure workability.*

![Fishery processing factories in Shiomi-cho]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kesennuma-ogawa-post/03.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 3: Fishery processing factories in Shiomi-cho*

The fishing industry requires direct access to the sea for mooring vessels, loading and unloading cargo, and transporting marine products. Therefore, coastal lowland locations are required despite the tsunami risk. At Kesennuma Fishing Port, the mooring and unloading functions have continued even after the disaster, with work routes secured in the limited quayside space between the vessels and the seawall built behind. At the fish market, a floodgate (a gate-type door installed in the seawall) has been installed to secure unloading routes to the quay while the landward seawall protects the urban area, achieving both disaster prevention and operational functionality. The fishery processing factories in Shiomi-cho have rebuilt their buildings on ground raised by land filling, reducing flood risk while maintaining proximity to the sea.

### 2.2 Sea Views and Disaster Prevention

![The seawall in Asahi-cho is built high, partly because the area is zoned as industrial.]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kesennuma-ogawa-post/04.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 4: The seawall in Asahi-cho is built high, partly because the area is zoned as industrial.*

![Information board for the flap gate seawall in Uo-machi]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kesennuma-ogawa-post/05.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 5: Information board for the flap gate seawall in Uo-machi*

Seawall design varies according to the character of each district. Asahi-cho is an industrial area concentrated with fishery processing and port operations, where few residents are present on a daily basis; accordingly, a tall, shielded seawall prioritizing protective performance has been constructed. In contrast, Uo-machi, located close to the central urban area, has adopted a flap gate seawall. In normal times, the wall body lies flat on the ground, opening up views to the sea; when a tsunami arrives, the body is raised upright by the buoyancy of the incoming water to provide protection. This minimizes the impact on the landscape while securing the necessary level of defense. Even within the category of "seawall," we can see that different forms are selected according to the character of the district and the impact on the living environment.

### 2.3 The Waterfront as a Space for Tourism and Exchange

![PIER7, developed integrally with the seawall. The name derives from its location at the 7th quay.]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kesennuma-ogawa-post/06.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 6: PIER7, developed integrally with the seawall. The name derives from its location at the 7th quay.*

In the inner bay area stretching from Minato-cho to Uohama-cho, tourism and exchange spaces have been developed based on the seawall and quay. PIER7 is a multipurpose public facility integrally designed with the seawall at the front of the 7th quay, featuring open views toward the sea. In Minamicho Kaigan Park, the top of the seawall has been developed as a promenade, creating a waterfront staying space. The design philosophy of not simply making the seawall a wall that separates the sea from the urban area, but rather layering vitality on top of and along the coast, has generated the coastal pedestrian circulation characteristic of this district.

### 2.4 Summary

Along Kesennuma's coast, different forms of coexistence with the sea are embodied in each district: continuation of the fishing industry through seawalls and floodgates (fishing port, fish market); balancing sea views with disaster prevention through flap gate seawalls (Uo-machi); and creation of vibrant spaces atop seawalls (northern inner bay). Seawalls are not uniformly installed as "walls blocking the sea," but rather their form is selected according to the use and living environment of each district. The diverse relationships with the sea—"place of production," "place for viewing," and "place for gathering"—are realized through meticulous design considerations.

## 3. Changes in Urban Structure for Coexistence with the Sea

### 3.1 Basic Concept of Post-Disaster Land Use

In the Great East Japan Earthquake, the lowlands along Kesennuma Bay were widely inundated, and much of the urban area suffered devastating damage. As the basic policy of the recovery plan, it was established that people should avoid "living" on coastal lowlands where tsunamis can repeatedly reach, and that such areas should be converted to non-residential uses. While residential functions were guided inland and to higher ground through disaster prevention group relocation projects and the designation of disaster-risk areas, a reorganization of land use was carried out to concentrate non-residential uses such as fishery industry, business, and tourism in the coastal lowlands. The concept of functional separation—"place to live (highland)" and "place to work and visit (coastal lowlands)"—forms the core of Kesennuma's recovery land use plan.

### 3.2 South: Hub for Fishery Processing and Port Operations

![The fishery processing and port operations hub on the south side, as seen from Kesennuma City Recovery Memorial Park]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kesennuma-ogawa-post/07.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 7: The fishery processing and port operations hub on the south side, as seen from Kesennuma City Recovery Memorial Park*

In the southern part of Kesennuma Bay (Asahi-cho, Shiomi-cho, Benten-cho, Uoichiba-mae), fishery processing, fishing port, and port logistics functions are concentrated. This area serves as the production and distribution center supporting Kesennuma's fishing industry. The entire sequence of operations—landing fish at the quay, auctioning at the fish market, and processing and shipping at adjacent cold storage and processing facilities—is structured to be completed largely within the southern area. These facilities are continuously located along the quay, and business land use that directly utilizes the sea is dominant.

### 3.3 Middle Section: Nexus Connecting the Fishing Industry and Tourism

From Uoichiba-mae to Minato-cho, the area serves as a nexus where movement routes for the fishing industry and tourism intersect. "Umi-no-ichi" (Sea Market), combining direct sales of marine products with dining facilities, is located in this area, where the operational routes of fishery workers and the arrival routes of tourists coexist in the same space. This zone functions as a buffer connecting the industrial and exchange hubs to the south and north.

### 3.4 North: Hub for Tourism, Exchange, Central Commerce, and Landscape Formation

![Mukaeru (迎: Welcome) and Umareru (創: Create), as seen from Kesennuma City Recovery Memorial Park]({{ '/assets/images/authors/kesennuma-ogawa-post/08.jpg' | relative_url }})
*Figure 8: Mukaeru (迎: Welcome) and Umareru (創: Create), as seen from Kesennuma City Recovery Memorial Park*

The northern part of Kesennuma Bay (Minamicho Kaigan, Uo-machi, Uohama-cho) has been redeveloped as a hub for tourism, exchange, and central commerce, utilizing the inner bay waterfront. Pedestrian routes are provided on top of and along the seawall, and commercial and exchange facilities such as Mukaeru (迎: Welcome) and Umareru (創: Create) are deployed by the waterfront. Land use oriented toward "viewing and gathering" at the sea forms the fundamental character of this district.

## 4. Summary

The functional arrangement along Kesennuma Bay forms a gradient from south to north: "Production (fishery processing, port) → Relay (intersection of industry and tourism) → Exchange (tourism, commerce)." The post-disaster recovery plan has intentionally reinforced this functional differentiation, and the relationship with the sea also transitions from the "sea as a place of production" in the south to the "sea for viewing and gathering" in the north.

This arrangement reflects Kesennuma's regional characteristics: protecting the production functions of a fishing city while utilizing the landscape resources of the inner bay for tourism and commerce. Coastal land use that coexists with the sea—rather than separating sea life from daily life—has been realized through the consensus-building between residents and the administration after the disaster, as well as through careful and meticulous design.
